Microphysics
1:15 - 1:40Andrew W. Steiner (UTK/ORNL) Hot and Dense Matter Equation of State Probability Distributions for Astrophysical Simulations
In this talk, I will present our new equation of state tables for core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers. I will describe the underlying nuclear physics, the improvements which they make on other currently available tables, and how these tables fit in the future of a few new and upcoming collaborations.
1:40 - 2:05Zidu Lin (UTK) Impact of EoS on Neutrino Opacities in Core-collapse supernovae
Neutrinos radiate 99% of the energy and play a crucial role in the explosion mechanism and nucleosynthesis of core-collapse supernovae. The neutrino interactions in hot and dense matter is a complex problem due to bound nuclei and the strong nuclear forces. In this work we calculate the dynamic and static response of the neutral current and charged current neutrino-nucleon interactions, from low-density region near the neutrino sphere to high-density region up to approximately 1.5 saturation densities at finite T, by applying random phase approximation (RPA). The consistency between an equation of state (EoS) and the neutrino opacity is guaranteed, by using density-dependent residual interactions, effective mass, single nucleon potentials directly derived from underlying EoSs in the RPA calculations. We further analyze the effect of EoSs on neutrino opacities by calculating the statistical uncertainties of neutrino opacities. The EoSs we used in this work naturally evolve to a model-independent virial EoS at low density, and are consistent with empirical constraints from (i) nuclear mass measurements, (ii) p-p scattering phase shifts, and (iii) neutron star observations. We found that our neutral current static response based on RPA is close to the results of model-independent virial approximation at low density, and the description of neutrino opacities at higher densities have large uncertainties due to still unclear strong (especially spin-dependent) nuclear forces.
2:05 - 2:30Zac Johnston (MSU) Comparison of updated electron-capture rates with core-collapse supernovae simulations
Previous studies have shown that the collapse and bounce of massive stellar cores is sensitive to electron-capture (EC) rates on nuclei near N=50. We have performed a large set of 1D supernova simulations using recently-updated rates in the N=50 region. By simulating to several seconds post-bounce, we are able to quantify the impact of different EC rate implementations on the long-term shock evolution, including the success or failure of explosion.
2:30 - 2:55 Luke Johns (UC Berkeley) Neutrino oscillations in supernovae: current status
This talk will summarize where and when neutrino flavor conversion is most likely to occur in core-collapse supernovae, and how its consequences might be ascertained. Particular emphasis will be placed on the recently discovered collisional instabilities.

Explosions/Signals I
3:15 - 3:40 David Vartanyan (UC Berkeley) Core-Collapse in 3D: Moving Beyond Spherically-Symmetric Single Stars
The recent advent of multi-dimensional core-collapse simulations have transformed the supernovae schema. However, the overwhelming majority of these new results are limited in that they rely on spherically-symmetric models of single-star supernovae progenitors. Most stars reside in binaries and experience strong binary interactions in their lifetimes. Such interactions significantly transform the progenitor profile on collapse, which alters the explosion probability and subsequent diagnostics. Separately, including self-consistently multi-dimensional effects to capture the nature and onset of turbulence in a pre-collapse model is of paramount importance. We report on one of the first self-consistent modeling and explosion of binary-stripped stars as well as of the explosion outcome of three-dimensional progenitors, taking advantage of recent groundbreaking work in this field. We find that binary stars lend themselves more handily to explosion, as do progenitor models evolved in 3D prior to collapse, and explore the ramifications for post-explosions diagnostics.
3:40 - 4:05 Michael Pajkos (MSU) Determining the Structure of Rotating Massive Stellar Cores with Gravitational Waves
The gravitational wave (GW) signal resulting from stellar core collapse encodes a wealth of information about the physical parameters of the progenitor star and the resulting core-collapse supernova (CCSN). This talk reviews recent work that leverages over 30 multidimensional simulations of stellar core collapse using the FLASH code, yielding a new analysis method to constrain the pre-collapse core compactness of the progenitor. Because the needed GW features occur less than a second after core bounce, this analysis could allow astronomers to predict electromagnetic properties of a resulting CCSN even before shock breakout. Likewise, this talk highlights recent additions to FLASH that account for dynamically evolving spacetimes.
4:05 - 4:30 Soumi De (LANL) Probing neutron star mergers with gravity and light
Observations of black hole and neutron star mergers in the era of multimessenger astronomy has enabled us to solve several problems in physics. LIGO-Virgoâ€™s first observations of a binary neutron merger, GW170817, was also the first ever event observed by both gravitational waves and light. Gravitational waves from compact binary coalescences encode information about physical properties of the component objects---such as masses, spins, and tidal deformabilities---in the source binary system. Measuring these parameters from neutron star merger observations can help us address several long-standing questions such as---what is the nature of matter at highest densities in the observable universe? The merger of two neutron stars or a neutron star and a black hole typically leads to the formation of a post-merger accretion disk. Outflows from such disks are a major source of r-process nuclei, fueling electromagnetic transients such as kilonovae. With gravitational-wave detectors starting to sample the neutron star merger parameter space, it is necessary to start producing models of the aftermath of mergers spanning the variety of neutron star binary populations. This can be used to study engines triggering electromagnetic transients from future observations. This talk will review what GW170817 has told us about properties of neutron stars and kilonova production after their merger. We will then explore what future observations might tell us.

Methods
1:00 - 1:25 Michael Zingale (SBU) The Continued Adventures of Coupling Reactions and Hydrodynamics
Traditional astrophysical hydrodynamics codes rely on operator / Strang splitting to couple reactions and hydrodynamics. If the reactions are vigorous, this coupling can breakdown. This can especially be true if the reactions are evolved without an energy-like equation. To enforce better coupling, we have developed a few approaches based around the ideas of spectral deferred corrections (SDC). For both Strang and SDC, we can encounter states that enter NSE during integration, so we implemented a hybrid reaction network / NSE method in the SDC framework. We discuss both the tradition method and the new ""simplified-SDC"" approach and show test problems comparing operator-splitting to the different SDC formulations and discuss the implications to massive star evolution.
1:25 - 1:50 Zack Elledge (UTK) Toward a General Relativistic Two-Moment, Spectral Kinetics Solver
I will report on the progress we have made to develop a general relativistic, two-moment, spectral neutrino kinetics solver for application to core collapse supernova modeling. I will discuss our theoretical formulation, numerical methods, the results of test problems successfully completed, and ongoing work, as well as near-term plans for the application of the solver.
1:50 - 2:15 Doreen Fan (LBL) Exploring neural networks for nuclear reactions in MAESTROeX
When coupling large-scale hydrodynamics to micro-scale nuclear reactions in various astrophysics applications, solving the nuclear reaction equations is oftentimes the single most computationally expensive step in a simulation. This is because the nucleosynthesis of a reacting fluid is described by a set of stiff, coupled ODEs that generally requires multiple implicit integrations to solve. To reduce the computational expense of large-scale astrophysical simulations, we consider replacing the ODE solver with a neural network surrogate model. In this talk, we will describe our approach to building a neural network to capture nucleosynthesis and energy generation in a low Mach number astrophysical flame using simulation data from MAESTROeX. We will also present the current and future integration of neural networks into the AMReX framework as an alternative to numerical solvers.
2:15 - 2:40 Johann Rudi (ANL) CodeFlow: A Code Generation System for Flash-X Orchestration Runtime
We propose the CodeFlow toolchain for Flash-X that realizes the ""recipe-to-source"" code transformation for Flash-X simulations and that is necessary to achieve performance portability. We design a high-level language to express operations of simulations in so-called recipes, which are given as input to the toolchain. The tools of the CodeFlow pipeline include code transformation with tree-based source code representation techniques and code orchestration and generation based on control flow graphs. The generated source code utilizes a new runtime, developed for Flash-X, that orchestrates dynamic and asynchronous data movement and task execution.

Explosions/Signals II
3:00 - 3:25 Carl Fields (LANL) Multidimensional Progenitor Models For Core-collapse Supernovae
Core-collapse supernova explosions (CCSN) are one possible fate of a massive star. Simulations of CCSNe rely on the properties of the massive star at core-collapse. As such, a critical component is the realization of realistic initial conditions. Multidimensional progenitor models can enable us to capture the chaotic nuclear shell burning occurring deep within the stellar interior. I will discuss ongoing efforts to progress our understanding of the nature of massive stars through next-generation hydrodynamic stellar models. In particular, I will present recent results of three-dimensional hydrodynamic massive star models evolved for the final 10 minutes before collapse. These recent results suggest that realistic 3D progenitor models can be favorable for obtaining robust models of CCSN explosions and are an important aspect of massive star explosions that must be taken into consideration.
3:25 - 3:50 Andre Sieverding (ORNL) Nucleosynthesis based on Multi-D simulations
I am going to show results from nucleosynthesis calculations based on tracer particles extracted from a three-dimensional supernova simulations and highlight some of the challenges that need to be addressed in order to obtain accurate nucleosynthesis predictions from state-of-the art supernova simulations.
3:50 - 4:15 Matt Coleman (Princeton) A Spattering of Core Collapse Supernovae Simulation Progress
I provide a brief update on various questions the Princeton group is trying to answer with our Fornax core collapse supernovae simulation code.
4:15 - 4:40 W. Raph Hix (ORNL/UTK) The hours between
The multi-messengers that herald the birth of a neutron star or black hole come widely separated in time, with the first photon signal trailing the neutrino and gravitational wave signals by many hours. We report here on simulations to bridge this gap, starting with world-class simulations of the neutrino-driven mechanism and ending just as the shock breaks the surface of the star.
